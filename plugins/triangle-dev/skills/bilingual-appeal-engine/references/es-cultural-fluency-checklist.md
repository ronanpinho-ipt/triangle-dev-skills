# Spanish Cultural-Fluency Checklist — US Latino Donor Communication

A working checklist for transcreating donor-facing copy into Spanish for **US Latino philanthropy**. The goal is copy that reads as if written by a fluent native, respects the donor, and carries the **same ask and CTA force** as the English. Use it inside Step 2 (Transcreate) and Step 3 (Parity QA) of the `bilingual-appeal-engine` skill.

> Principle: **transcreate, don't translate.** A literal translation that is grammatically correct can still feel foreign, transactional, or condescending. This checklist catches the gaps a dictionary won't.

---

## 1. Register & formality — *usted* vs *tú*

The single highest-impact choice. Get it wrong and even perfect grammar reads as either cold or presumptuous.

- **Default to *usted*** for donor appeals to a mixed or unknown audience. It signals respect (*respeto*) and is the safe formal-warm baseline for asking someone for money. Most US Latino donors will never be offended by *usted*.
- **Use *tú*** only when the audience is genuinely peer-level, youthful, or a community where the org has an established informal relationship (e.g., program participants, a youth volunteer base). *Tú* read as warmth there; *usted* would read as distant.
- **Never mix within one piece.** Register drift (opening in *usted*, slipping into *tú* commands like "dona ya") breaks fluency. Lock the choice and conjugate consistently: *usted* → "done", "su apoyo", "gracias por estar"; *tú* → "dona", "tu apoyo", "gracias por estar".
- **Avoid *vosotros* entirely.** It is Peninsular Spain; in the US it reads as foreign/incorrect. Use *ustedes* for plural-you in all cases.
- **Imperative softening:** a formal ask can stay direct without being harsh — "Únase hoy" / "Done antes del 31" are warm and clear in *usted*. Don't over-soften into passive distance ("Se le invita a considerar...") — that kills CTA force.

**QA flag:** register chosen explicitly, justified in cultural notes, and 100% consistent.

---

## 2. Regional neutrality — write "neutral American Spanish"

US Latino audiences are multi-origin (Mexican, Puerto Rican, Cuban, Dominican, Salvadoran, Guatemalan, Colombian, Venezuelan, and more). Default to **neutral, pan-regional Spanish** unless the brief targets one specific community.

- **Pick the widely-understood word, not the regional one.** Neutral choices that travel across communities:
  - "car" → **auto / carro** (avoid *coche* if you want max neutrality; *coche* is fine in MX but means other things elsewhere).
  - "kids/children" → **niños / hijos** (neutral) rather than regional slang (*chamacos*, *guaguas*, *pibes*, *chavos*).
  - "money" → **dinero / fondos** (neutral); avoid *plata*, *lana*, *pasta* in formal appeals.
  - "right now" → **ahora mismo / hoy** rather than *ahorita* (which means very different time-spans by region).
- **Watch words that are innocent in one country and vulgar/comedic in another.** Classic traps: *coger* (neutral "to take/grab" in Spain/much of S. America, but vulgar in MX and parts of Central America — use *tomar*, *agarrar*, *recoger* instead). *Pisto*, *bicho*, *concha*, *pija* and similar carry strong regional/vulgar meanings — avoid entirely in donor copy.
- **Currency & numbers:** US donors → keep **US dollars**, written `$100`. Use the format your audience reads in the US; do not switch to European decimal commas for a US-facing piece. Be internally consistent.
- **Dates:** spell out to avoid MM/DD vs DD/MM confusion — "el 31 de diciembre", not "12/31".

**QA flag:** run a regionalism + vulgar-meaning scan; no term flagged for any major US-Latino community.

## 3. False-cognate traps (high-frequency offenders)

These wreck fluency because they're *almost* right. Common ones in fundraising copy:

| English | Tempting (wrong) | Correct Spanish |
|---|---|---|
| actually | actualmente (= currently) | en realidad / de hecho |
| eventually | eventualmente (= possibly/by chance) | con el tiempo / finalmente |
| to support (a cause) | soportar (= to endure/tolerate) | apoyar / respaldar |
| to realize (understand) | realizar (= to carry out) | darse cuenta |
| sensible (sensitive) | sensible (= sensitive) | sensato (for "sensible/reasonable") |
| to introduce (a person/idea) | introducir (= insert) | presentar |
| application (to apply) | aplicación (software/app) | solicitud |
| advice/notice | aviso vs. consejo | aviso = notice; consejo = advice |
| contribution (donation) | — | aportación / aporte / donativo (all natural) |
| fund / fundraising | fondear (colloquial) | recaudar fondos / recaudación |
| compromise | compromiso (= commitment) | acuerdo / concesión |
| to attend (an event) | atender (= to assist/serve) | asistir a |
| current (present) | corriente (= ordinary/flowing) | actual |

**QA flag:** scanned the draft for these; "support / apoyar" and "donation / donativo·aportación" are the two most common in appeals — verify them explicitly.

## 4. Culturally-resonant framing of giving

Translate the *meaning of giving*, not just the words. US Latino giving culture skews toward **family, community, reciprocity, faith, and dignity** — and away from cold individual-transaction or guilt-based framing.

- **Frame around comunidad and familia**, not the lone individual. "Together / juntos", "nuestra comunidad", "como familia" outperform purely individualistic "you alone can…". 
- **Lead with dignity and pride, not pity.** Avoid copy that positions beneficiaries as helpless. Use *apoyar*, *acompañar*, *sumar* (to add/join) — agency words — over savior framing.
- **Reciprocity and confianza.** Trust (*confianza*) is currency. Naming the relationship ("gracias por ser parte de…", "su confianza en nosotros") lands harder than a bare transactional ask.
- **Faith-adjacent warmth, used carefully.** Concepts like *bendición*, *fe en la comunidad*, *esperanza* resonate broadly — but only deploy explicit religious language if the org's voice and audience support it. Don't assume.
- **Honor multigenerational giving.** Many Latino households give as a family unit, sometimes honoring elders or future generations ("por las próximas generaciones", "en honor a quienes nos abrieron el camino"). Strong when the campaign fits.
- **Avoid:** guilt-tripping, scarcity-panic that reads as manipulative, savior/charity-from-above tone, or "exotic"/stereotyped flourishes (no gratuitous "¡Olé!", no assumed nationality, no decorative Spanglish).

**QA flag:** the giving ask reads as an invitation to belong/contribute with dignity, and carries the same emotional weight as the English — not flatter, not preachier.

---

## 5. Parity of ask & CTA across languages

The whole point: a beautiful Spanish appeal that quietly weakens the ask is a failed deliverable.

- **Same dollar amount / same ladder.** `$100` in EN = `$100` in ES; "$50/$100/$250" ladder carries over identically.
- **Same deadline and same mechanism.** Same date, same link/URL, same "monthly vs one-time" structure.
- **CTA equal in force and directness.** The Spanish button/line must be as imperative and motivating as the English. Good formal CTAs: "Done hoy", "Únase ahora", "Haga su aporte", "Sume su apoyo antes del 31". Don't downgrade a punchy English CTA into a polite suggestion.
- **No added hedging.** Spanish politeness conventions can tempt extra softening ("si usted gustara considerar…") — resist it; keep the ask clean.
- **Subject lines / preheaders / button text** count as parity items too — transcreate them, don't drop or shorten them.
- **Placeholders stay placeholders.** Any `<VERIFY: ...>` in the English (amount, statistic, name) remains a `<VERIFY: ...>` in the Spanish. Never "fill in" a value during transcreation. **Zero fabrication applies in both languages.**

**QA flag:** every ask/CTA element mapped 1:1 in the parity table with ✅ before the deliverable ships.

---

## Quick pre-ship pass (run all)

1. Register (*usted*/*tú*) chosen, justified, consistent — no *vosotros*.
2. Neutral Spanish; no regionalism or vulgar-in-some-country term.
3. False cognates scanned — especially *apoyar* (support) and *donativo/aportación* (donation).
4. Giving framed with community + dignity, equal emotional weight to EN.
5. Ask, deadline, link, and CTA at full parity and full force.
6. All `<VERIFY: ...>` placeholders intact in both languages; nothing fabricated.
